What actually works when you revise
Re-reading your notes feels productive but research consistently shows it is one of the weakest study methods. These work better.
1
Close the book and write what you remember
Read a topic. Close your notes. Write down everything you can recall. Check. Fill the gaps. This is called active recall and it is the single most effective revision technique. It feels harder than re-reading — that is the point.
2
Space it out — do not cram
Studying a topic today and then again in three days and again in a week builds stronger memory than doing all three sessions in one night. The revision program is designed to help you do this across eight weeks.
3
25 minutes on, 5 minutes off
Set a timer for 25 minutes. Do nothing but study. When it goes off, take a proper 5-minute break — stand up, move, look away from your screen. Then repeat. This is called the Pomodoro technique and it makes long revision sessions much more manageable.
4
Explain it to someone else
Try to explain a concept — the business life cycle, what a stakeholder is — out loud to a family member, a friend, or even just to yourself. If you cannot explain it simply, you do not fully understand it yet. This is one of the best ways to find the gaps.
5
Do the exam practice questions
The Weekend tasks in the revision program are exam simulations for a reason. Writing under timed conditions — no notes, no phone, just the question and your book — is the closest thing to the real exam. The more times you do it before the day, the less frightening the day will be.
6
Look after yourself
Sleep, food and movement are not optional extras you sacrifice when exams get close — they are part of your preparation. A tired brain retains and retrieves information poorly. Revising at 2am the night before rarely helps as much as a full night of sleep.
