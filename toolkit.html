Section 1
Command Verb Guide
These are the verbs NESA uses in exam questions. The verb tells you exactly how much to write and how deep to go. Click any card to see the full definition and what examiners look for.
Surface — name and state
Moderate — features and structure
Deep — argument and judgement
Border colour shows depth only — mark values depend on the specific exam question.
Identify ▼
Use when: you just need to name something.
NESA definition
Recognise and name.
What examiners look for
One correct term or name. No explanation needed — adding one doesn’t earn extra marks, but it wastes your time. Keep it short.
Example question
Identify the legal structure of the business described in the stimulus. (1 mark)
Define ▼
Use when: you need to state what something means.
NESA definition
State meaning and identify essential qualities.
What examiners look for
A complete definition that captures the essential features. Do not pad with examples unless the question asks for them. One clear sentence is usually enough.
Example question
Define the term ‘goodwill’. (1 mark)
Outline ▼
Use when: you need to cover the main points without going deep.
NESA definition
Sketch in general terms; indicate the main features of.
What examiners look for
Broad coverage, not deep explanation. Think “wide not tall.” Name and briefly state each key feature. You do not need to explain why — save that for Explain questions.
Example question
Outline the features of a sole trader as a legal structure. (3 marks)
Describe ▼
Use when: you need to paint a full picture of what something is or looks like.
NESA definition
Provide characteristics and features.
What examiners look for
More detail per feature than an outline, but still not a full cause-and-effect explanation. Think: what does this look like in practice? What are its characteristics?
Example question
Describe the challenges a business is likely to face during the maturity stage of the business life cycle. (4 marks)
Distinguish ▼
Use when: two things need to be directly compared by their differences.
NESA definition
Note points of difference.
What examiners look for
Direct contrast. Use language like “whereas,” “unlike,” and “in contrast.” Do not define each term separately — that is not distinguishing. The comparison must be explicit.
Example question
Distinguish between voluntary and involuntary cessation of a business. (3 marks)
Explain ▼
Use when: you need to show why or how something happens.
NESA definition
Relate cause and effect; make relationships between things evident; provide why and/or how.
What examiners look for
The word “because” should appear (or be clearly implied). Show the link between a cause and its effect. A common mistake is to describe instead of explain — always go one step further and say why or how.
Example question
Explain how an increase in interest rates could affect a small business that has taken out a variable-rate bank loan. (4 marks)
Justify ▼
Use when: you need to defend a conclusion with reasons.
NESA definition
Support an argument or conclusion.
What examiners look for
State your position clearly, then back it up with reasoning or evidence. “Because” + “as a result” + “therefore” are useful connectors. You are not asked to consider the other side — just support your conclusion.
Example question
Justify why a business owner with limited industry experience might choose to buy a franchise rather than start a new business. (3 marks)
Discuss ▼
Use when: you need to explore both sides of an argument.
NESA definition
Identify issues and provide points for and/or against.
What examiners look for
Both sides of the argument. Acknowledge complexity. Come to a reasoned conclusion. One-sided responses lose marks at the higher mark levels. Do not just list points — connect them and reach a position.
Example question
“The maturity stage is the most dangerous stage of the business life cycle.” Discuss this statement with reference to the stimulus. (6 marks)
Assess ▼
Use when: you need to weigh something up and make a judgement.
NESA definition
Make a judgement of value, quality, outcomes, results or size.
What examiners look for
Do not just describe — you must arrive at a judgement. Weigh the evidence and state how significant, effective or important something is. Your conclusion must be supported by the points you have made.
Example question
Assess the importance of a well-developed business plan to the long-term success of an SME. (6 marks)
Evaluate ▼
Use when: you need to judge something against clear criteria.
NESA definition
Make a judgement based on criteria; determine the value of.
What examiners look for
Similar to assess, but implies you are measuring against stated criteria. Set up what “success” or “effectiveness” looks like, then judge the evidence against it. A strong conclusion is essential — do not leave the reader uncertain of your position.
Example question
Evaluate the effectiveness of the corrective actions taken by a business in response to falling sales performance. (6 marks)

Verb definitions are taken word-for-word from the NESA HSC examination glossary of key words. Depth labels (Surface / Moderate / Deep) are a general guide only — they are not official NESA categories. Actual mark values depend on the specific exam question.

Question Sparks
Click any card to open the full question. Use the filters to find a type that suits you right now. These are extras — there is no obligation to do all of them.
Quick Fire
Five industry sectors — from memory
Tap to open →
In your revision book, without looking at your notes:

List the five industry sectors in order. For each, write one sentence describing what it involves and name one example business.

Sectors: Primary · Secondary · Tertiary · Quaternary · Quinary

Check your answers against your notes when you are done.
Quick Fire
ABS size classifications — exact thresholds
Tap to open →
Without looking at your notes:

Write the four ABS business size categories and the number of employees that defines each one.

Then write one example of a real business for each category.

This is a 1–2 mark recall question in disguise — if you cannot recall the thresholds, you will lose marks even if your explanation is strong.
Quick Fire
Business life cycle — four stages and key features
Tap to open →
Name the four stages of the business life cycle. For each stage, write two key features a business is likely to show at that point.

Then answer this in one sentence: What is the most important thing a business can do at the post-maturity stage, and why does it matter?

Aim to do this in under 10 minutes.
Quick Fire
SWOT — internal vs external
Tap to open →
Without looking at your notes: What does each letter of SWOT stand for? Which elements are internal to the business and which are external?

Then write one example of each for a small bakery in a regional town.

Common exam mistake: students confuse threats and weaknesses. Can you explain in one sentence what makes them different?
Quick Fire
Debt vs equity finance
Tap to open →
Define debt finance and equity finance. Give two examples of each. Then explain one advantage and one disadvantage of each type.

Finally, answer: If a new business owner wants to avoid giving up control of the business, which type of finance should they prefer and why?
Quick Fire
Internal and external influences — sort and classify
Tap to open →
In your revision book, draw two columns: Internal Influences and External Influences.

Sort the following into the correct column and write the specific type of influence (economic, legal, social, technological, etc.) next to each:

Rising interest rates · A change in the owner’s vision · New workplace safety legislation · A key staff member resigns · Growing consumer interest in sustainable products · A new competitor opens nearby · The business invests in new software · The owner runs out of personal savings
Scenario
Woolworths Group — size and geographical spread
Tap to open →
Woolworths Group is listed on the Australian Securities Exchange, employs over 200,000 people across Australia and New Zealand, and operates supermarkets, discount stores and liquor retail chains.

(a) Classify Woolworths Group by size using ABS definitions. Justify your answer. (2 marks)

(b) Classify Woolworths Group by geographical spread. Justify your answer. (2 marks)

(c) Identify one role of business demonstrated by Woolworths Group. Use the information above to support your answer. (2 marks)
Scenario
Sales plateau — identify the life cycle stage
Tap to open →
After three years of rapid growth, a Sydney café group’s monthly revenue has been flat for eight months. Two larger competitors have opened in three of its key suburbs, and regular customers are beginning to try the new venues.

(a) Identify the most likely stage of the business life cycle. Justify your answer with reference to the information above. (2 marks)

(b) Suggest two strategies the owner could use to respond to this stage. For each strategy, explain how it would help the business. (4 marks)
Scenario
Interest rate rise — impact on a small business
Tap to open →
The Reserve Bank of Australia raises the official cash rate. A small furniture manufacturer has a $180,000 variable-rate bank loan used to purchase equipment. The owner also relies on consumer spending for sales.

Explain how the interest rate rise could affect this business. In your answer, refer to at least two different effects. (4 marks)

Hint: think beyond just the loan repayment — consider what the rate rise signals about the broader economy.
Scenario
Store closures — stakeholder conflict
Tap to open →
A national supermarket chain announces it will close four regional stores and consolidate into larger suburban locations. The decision is driven by falling profitability in those stores.

Explain how this decision creates a conflict between two different stakeholder groups. For each stakeholder group, explain what their interest is and how the decision affects them. Suggest one way the business could try to address the conflict. (6 marks)
Scenario
Court-appointed liquidator — cessation type
Tap to open →
A construction company cannot meet its debt obligations. A court appoints a liquidator who sells the company’s assets and property to repay creditors. The business then ceases to exist.

(a) Identify the type of cessation this represents. (1 mark)

(b) Explain the difference between this type of cessation and voluntary cessation. In your answer, refer to the consequences for the business owner in each case. (4 marks)
Verb Drill
Break-even point at three verb levels
Tap to open →
Write three separate responses about break-even point, one at each verb level. Keep each to the right length for its verb.

(a) Define break-even point. (1 mark)

(b) Outline how the break-even point is calculated and what it tells a business owner. (3 marks)

(c) Explain why understanding the break-even point is important during the establishment phase of a business. (4 marks)

After writing all three, look at them side by side. What had to change as the verb got deeper?
Verb Drill
Stakeholders — from identify to discuss
Tap to open →
Use the topic of stakeholders to practise writing at different depths.

(a) Identify two stakeholders of a large public company. (1 mark)

(b) Outline the interests of three different stakeholders of a large public company. (3 marks)

(c) Explain how the interests of shareholders and employees may conflict when a business decides to cut costs by reducing its workforce. (4 marks)

(d) Discuss whether a business has a responsibility to prioritise its employees over its shareholders. (6 marks)
Verb Drill
Outline vs Explain — same topic, different depth
Tap to open →
Choose ONE of the following topics: legal structures · external influences · business life cycle stages.

Step 1: Write an outline response about your chosen topic. (3 marks)

Step 2: Now rewrite it as an explain response. (4 marks)

Step 3: Highlight or underline every word or phrase you added in Step 2 that was not in Step 1. What type of language did you add?

This exercise trains you to switch depth automatically when you read a command verb.
Verb Drill
Create your own questions
Tap to open →
Pick any concept from your revision notes. Write four questions about it — one for each of these verbs:

Identify · Outline · Explain · Discuss

Then swap with a classmate and attempt each other’s questions.

Writing exam questions is one of the best revision strategies there is. If you can write a good question, you understand the content.
Fix It
Weak answer: defining stakeholders
Tap to open →
Question: Outline the interests of THREE different stakeholders of a large supermarket chain. (3 marks)

Student answer:
“Stakeholders are people interested in the business, like customers and shareholders. Customers want good products and shareholders want money.”
This answer scores 1 out of 3.

Your tasks:
(a) Identify at least two things that are wrong or missing from this answer.
(b) Rewrite the answer to score full marks.
Fix It
Weak answer: maturity stage
Tap to open →
Question: Describe TWO challenges a business typically faces during the maturity stage of the business life cycle. (4 marks)

Student answer:
“In the maturity stage the business stops growing and things are quite stable. There is competition and it can be hard to keep customers.”
This answer scores 1 out of 4.

Your tasks:
(a) Explain why this answer is so weak despite being on the right track.
(b) Rewrite the answer to score 4 marks.
Fix It
Weak answer: voluntary vs involuntary cessation
Tap to open →
Question: Explain the difference between voluntary and involuntary cessation of a business. In your answer, give one example of each. (4 marks)

Student answer:
“Voluntary cessation is when a business closes because it wants to. Involuntary cessation is when it has to close. For example a business might retire or go bankrupt.”
This answer scores 1 out of 4.

Your tasks:
(a) List what is missing from this answer.
(b) Write a full response that would score 4 marks.
Fix It
Weak answer: recommending a legal structure
Tap to open →
Question: A sole trader is considering changing the legal structure of their business to a private company. Explain TWO advantages this change would offer. (4 marks)

Student answer:
“A private company is better because it gives more options and is more professional. It also lets you get more money from investors which is good for the business.”
This answer scores 0 out of 4.

Your tasks:
(a) Explain why this answer scores zero. Be specific.
(b) Write a response that would score 4 marks. Use the word “because” at least twice.
Challenge
Convince me: the business plan after opening
Tap to open →
A classmate says: “The business plan is only useful when you’re starting up. Once you’re open, you just react to what happens.”

Write a two-paragraph response that argues against this view.

Your response must include at least one specific business function (operations, marketing, finance or HR) and one specific planning tool (SWOT, break-even, cash flow forecast, etc.).

You have 10 minutes. Write in full sentences as if this is an exam.
Challenge
Rank the five sectors: most to least vulnerable
Tap to open →
Rank the five industry sectors from most to least vulnerable during an economic downturn.

Sectors: Primary · Secondary · Tertiary · Quaternary · Quinary

For each position in your ranking, write one sentence justifying why you placed it there.

There is no single correct answer — what matters is that your reasoning is consistent and well-supported. Compare your ranking with a classmate and discuss any differences.
Challenge
Pick a real business — which life cycle stage is it in?
Tap to open →
Choose any real Australian business you know.

Write a short paragraph (4–6 sentences) that:
• Names the business and identifies which life cycle stage you believe it is currently in
• Gives at least two pieces of evidence from what you know about the business to support your answer
• Suggests one challenge the business is likely facing at this stage

Ideas: Bunnings, Qantas, Afterpay, a local business you know, a brand that has recently expanded or closed stores.
Challenge
Advise a friend: which establishment option?
Tap to open →
A friend has $90,000 to invest in a business. She has strong communication skills, no specific industry experience, and wants to start generating income within six months. She does not want to take on a lot of personal liability.

Recommend an establishment option for her. You may not recommend “starting from scratch.”

Write your recommendation in 3–5 sentences. You must refer to at least two factors from the description above and explain why your recommendation suits her situation better than the other option.
Challenge
Is the business life cycle just a theory?
Tap to open →
A classmate says: “The business life cycle is just a theory — real businesses don’t follow it. Some skip stages, some go straight from growth to closure.”

Write a two-paragraph response. In the first paragraph, acknowledge the strongest point in your classmate’s argument. In the second, explain why the life cycle model is still a useful tool for business owners despite its limitations.

This is a “discuss” style question in disguise. Notice how presenting both sides before reaching a conclusion is exactly what the discuss verb requires.
